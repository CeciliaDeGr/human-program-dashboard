Energy and Efficiency in Cells

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Define biological energy.
  • Explain the role of ATP in storing cellular energy.
  • Calculate efficiency using an energy equation.
  • Interpret simple energy calculations in biological systems.

1. What Is Energy?

Energy is the capacity to perform work or cause change. In biological systems, energy allows organisms to maintain order, grow, reproduce, and respond to their environment.

Living cells constantly transform energy from one form to another.

Examples of cellular activities that require energy include:

  • muscle contraction
  • transport of molecules across membranes
  • synthesis of proteins
  • cell division
"Life depends on continuous energy transformation." — a central principle in biology

Definition

Energy

The ability of a system to perform work or produce change.

2. ATP — The Energy Currency of the Cell

Cells store usable energy primarily in a molecule called ATP (adenosine triphosphate).

ATP contains three phosphate groups. When one phosphate bond is broken, energy is released for cellular work.

The basic ATP reaction can be represented as:

ATP → ADP + P + energy

Approximately 30.5 kJ/mol of energy is released during this reaction.

Concept

ATP and cellular energy

ATP functions like a rechargeable battery. Energy is stored when ATP is formed. Energy is released when ATP is broken into ADP. Cells constantly recycle ATP molecules.

3. Energy Efficiency

Not all energy in biological reactions becomes useful work. Some energy is always lost as heat.

Scientists measure this using the concept of efficiency.

The efficiency equation is:

Efficiency = (Useful Energy Output ÷ Total Energy Input) × 100

Examples

Worked example

A biological process receives 500 joules of energy from glucose. Only 150 joules are converted into useful ATP energy.

Step 1: Insert the values into the formula

Efficiency = (150 ÷ 500) × 100

Step 2: Solve

Efficiency = 30%

Therefore, the reaction is 30% efficient.

Muscle efficiency

Human muscles convert chemical energy into movement with an efficiency of roughly 20–25%. The remaining energy is released as heat, which helps regulate body temperature.

4. Energy Stored in Food

Food molecules contain stored chemical energy. One of the most important molecules is glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆).

During cellular respiration, glucose reacts with oxygen to release energy.

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy

This process allows cells to produce approximately:

  • 36–38 ATP molecules per glucose molecule

Did you know?

ATP recycling in the body

Your body uses and regenerates its own weight in ATP molecules every day. However, ATP is constantly recycled rather than stored in large quantities.

5. Why Energy Is Lost

Energy loss occurs because biological systems obey the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

This law states that every energy transfer increases the overall disorder of the universe.

As a result, some energy becomes unavailable for useful work and is released as heat.

Key Points

  • Energy powers all biological processes.
  • ATP is the main molecule used to store cellular energy.
  • Energy transfer in cells is never perfectly efficient.
  • Efficiency can be calculated using an energy ratio.
  • Some energy is always lost as heat.

Vocabulary

ATP

A molecule that stores and transfers energy in living cells.

Efficiency

The percentage of input energy converted into useful output.

Cellular Respiration

A process that converts glucose into usable energy in the form of ATP.
